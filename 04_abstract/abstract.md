# Abstract

The escalating integration of machine learning in higher education offers transformative potential for predicting student success and identifying at-risk individuals early in their academic journey. Proactive Early Warning Systems (EWS) are increasingly essential for institutions to reduce dropout rates and support Sustainable Development Goals for equitable education. However, traditional Requirements Engineering (RE) methodologies, designed for deterministic logic, are often inadequate for the probabilistic and data-driven nature of AI components. This paradigm shift leads to high project failure rates, as current frameworks struggle to address "hallucinations," algorithmic bias, and the lack of technical specifications for non-functional requirements such as explainability and ethics. This study conducts a systematic literature review to synthesize existing research on RE for AI-based EWS. It aims to identify methodological gaps and provide practical insights for specifying and validating requirements tailored to students, lecturers, and academic advisors. The research analyzes high-quality peer-reviewed articles and tertiary studies within the SE4AI and Learning Analytics domains, categorizing findings according to the SWEBOK framework and socio-technical systems theory. The analysis reveals that while advanced models like Neural Networks and Ensemble methods achieve high predictive accuracy (AUC > 0.85), their effectiveness depends on integrating diverse data sources, including LMS behavioral traces and administrative records. Crucially, explainability, fairness, and trust are identified as the most critical but under-specified non-functional requirements in current educational AI deployments. The study concludes that RE must serve as the coordinating cornerstone to bridge the gap between data scientists and educational stakeholders. By establishing a robust RE process that emphasizes the prediction-intervention-evaluation cycle, institutions can ensure that AI systems are not only algorithmically precise but also pedagogically sound, ethical, and human-centered.

**Keywords:** Requirements Engineering, AI-based System, Early Warning System, Higher Education, Explainability, Non-Functional Requirements.
